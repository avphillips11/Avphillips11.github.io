The Problem

One person. An entire program.

As the primary owner of a full GRC and Privacy program at a leading cybersecurity company, the scope is substantial: SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001/27701, PCI DSS, NIST 800-171, GDPR, EU AI Act, ISO 42001, third-party risk management, privacy operations, and a multi-workstream risk program revamp, with a team of two.

Multiple audits run simultaneously. Frameworks overlap but don't align cleanly. Evidence collection is manual. Stakeholder reporting requires assembling data from a half-dozen systems. Strategic initiatives (the ones that actually mature the program) compete for time with daily operational load.

I needed a way to scale capacity without scaling headcount.

The Approach

A structured AI team

In April 2026, I built Project TOG, a structured AI team workspace running inside Claude Code. Rather than using AI as a generic assistant, I designed a team of 13 distinct specialists, each with a defined role, model assignment, and domain expertise. I direct work to them by name, the same way I'd delegate to human colleagues. A model tiering strategy matches AI capability to role complexity, cutting costs by ~51% versus an all-Opus deployment.

The core design principle: the AI orchestrator never does work directly. All tasks are routed to the right specialist. If no specialist exists, a hire pipeline triggers: a research agent investigates what the role should look like, an HR agent designs the persona and onboards them, and then the work begins.

After Project TOG

  • Risk program: full current-state assessment and 8-workstream plan from real data

  • Weekly reporting: automated KPI dashboard refreshes every Thursday. Zero manual effort.
  • Risk ticket creation: ~5 minutes. Automated scoring, controls mapping, and treatment plans.

Methodology

Why this works

Specialization over generality. A generic AI assistant produces generic outputs. By giving each team member a specific domain, model assignment, and working context, the outputs are calibrated to the right level of depth and use the right vocabulary. Dorian doesn't explain what SOC 2 is. Chaol doesn't define FAIR. Gavriel doesn't summarize the EU AI Act at a beginner level. They work at the level a senior practitioner expects.

Model tiering as cost discipline. Fable for deep analytical reasoning (governance, research). Opus for complex multi-step work (risk, compliance). Sonnet for structured execution. The result: ~51% cost reduction versus an all-Opus deployment, with no drop in output quality.

Live data, not stale descriptions. By connecting to production systems via MCP (Jira, Confluence, Slack, Vanta, Zoom), the AI team works from real data, not a summary provided from memory. When Chaol assessed the risk program, he read the actual risk register. When Fenrys built the KPI dashboard, he queried 9 live Jira data sources.

Human-in-the-loop by design. Every deliverable lands in a Review folder. Nothing is "done" until reviewed. The system is designed for AI-assisted work, not AI-autonomous work. I am the program manager; the AI team extends my capacity, not my authority.
